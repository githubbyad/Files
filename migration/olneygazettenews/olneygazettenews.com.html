            Barbara J. Beck, 83
            Barbara J. Beck, 83, of Sturgeon Bay, Wis., passed away early Monday, March 20, 2023, at Door County Medical Center. Barbara was born October 10, 1939, in Olney, the daughter of the late (Gould) Weber and Evelyn Jane (Greathouse) Murvin. Barbara graduated from West Richland High School before entering Northwestern University, graduating from Dental Hygiene School. On September 23, 1962, she married John Jay Beck in Olney. Barb continued to work as a dental hygienist until the birth of her first of four children in 1964. She would devote her life to her family, instilling in her children her love of adventure and food. Barb was a great driver, and at a moment’s notice gathered the children and headed out for learning adventures and sights to see with a mission to expose them to the world outside Door County. She loved to read novels, and was an accomplished and learned cook, deciphering recipes and cookbooks, leaving many notes and observations throughout the margins, serving as a lasting tribute to her culinary interests and devotion to cooking for her family. Her collection of 19 sewing machines was a complement to her sewing interests, creative talents, and mechanical abilities as she was able to fix those machines when needed. Surviving family include her devoted husband, Dr. John Beck, of Sturgeon Bay, Wis.; four loving children, John (Elizabeth) Beck, of Appleton, Wis., Dan (Rose) Beck, of Algoma, Wis., Laura Beck, of Madison, Wis., and Martha Delancey, of Green Bay, Wis.; six precious grandchildren, John Howard Beck, Jeremy Badgley Beck, Oliver Caspian Delancey, Edmund Nikolas Delancey, Amy Sue Beck, and Chad Roland Beck; brother, Roger Murvin, of the Villages, Fla.; and sister, Margaret (Dan) Shimkos, of Okemos, Mich. Preceding her in death were her parents, brother, Stephen Murvin, and sister-in-law, Mary Murvin. Private family burial will be held in Bayside Cemetery, Sturgeon Bay, Wis. A celebration of her life and gathering will be held from noon-3 p.m. Thursday, April 6 at The Lodge at Leathem Smith, 1640 Memorial Dr., Sturgeon Bay, WI 54235. “Our mother was everything to us, from the daily fresh baked sour dough bread and sewing our clothes to giving us a solid foundation of family life and exposing us to the world we would live in. The love she and our dad had for each other was powerful and was evident as she supported him and his devotion to the community he served. She was a great example for all who knew her and we will miss her deeply.” Huehns Funeral Home, Inc. in Sturgeon Bay, Wis. is assisting the Beck family. Expressions of sympathy, memories, and photos of Barbara may be shared with her family through her tribute page at www.huehnsfuneralhome.com.

            Carle Health names Knight chief medical officer
            Carle Health announced Napoleon Knight, MD, as its new chief medical officer. Knight has more than 30 years of experience as an Emergency Medicine physician and other leadership roles as he steers Carle’s efforts to empower all clinicians to provide high-quality outcomes for patients. He most recently held the role of medical director of the Carle southern region Emergency Department, and also led as vice president of Medical Affairs, medical director of the Rural Alliance, and several other positions in the health-care system. Dr. Knight has extensive understanding of the needs of the patient population and the challenges of the evolving medical industry. He excels at bringing innovation and collaboration to the forefront to help create timely solutions. In his many leadership roles in the Carle Health system, he worked to implement strategies to improve the health of the diverse service population, extend specialty care available in the system, increase health-plan membership, and made lasting impact to advancing the quality standards of the organization. He also serves as a clinical associate professor at the first-of-its-kind Carle Illinois College of Medicine. As an involved member of the medical community, he spent time as the American Association for Physician Leadership Board chairman, and is a board member of the Illinois College of Emergency Physicians. Knight earned a bachelor’s degree from the University of Colorado, Boulder, Colo., medical degree from the University of Minnesota, Minneapolis, completed an Emergency Medicine Residency, Denver General Affiliated Program in Emergency Medicine, and an MBA, University of Massachusetts, Amherst, Mass.

            John William “Bill” Schwartz, 81, of Olney, passed away Friday, June 5, 2020, at his home with his loving family and beloved pet Little John Pepper at his side.             Council crawls over finish line, approves golf carts and UTVs on city streets on 3-2 vote
            Mark Allen Editor Olney City Council approved street use of golf carts and UTVs at its meeting Nov. 25 on a 3-2 vote. Mirroring the multi-meeting debate on the issue, the council navigated a surreal landscape paved with (at least) two page twos and five page fives, including one erroneous copy that mischievously tagged along. City Attorney Bart Zuber gave the council multiple options, including for carts, UTVs, and both. Councilwoman Belinda Henton said someone asked her how many times cars were inspected before a licenses are issued. If a cart or UTV passed an initial inspection, it would probably be the same the following year, she said, and police could issue citations if not. One-time inspections would not impose as much burden on police, Henton said. “It’s time they can meet an officer in a good way,” she said, adding that officers were posted at basketball games for visibility. Mayor Mark Lambird asked who would do the inspections. Henton said police could perform the 10-15 inspections she expected, eliminating the extra fee and a burden on local businesses. Councilman John McLaughlin said he was fine either way. Lambird said he could see the benefit of inspections by certified professionals, but the council could revisit the issue if 200 people apply. “I don’t feel like we need to add more for the chief to do and his staff. I think they’re at their limit,” said Councilwoman Morgan Fehrenbacher. Lambird asked how often the police department had someone there who could readily perform an inspection. “I dunno. We can do them. Whatever,” said Police Chief Kevin Paddock. Lambird asked at what point it would be a burden due to not having officers at the station. Paddock said they would probably have to set up an appointment schedule. “We ran all day today.” Councilman Greg Eyer quoted an insurance agent that the ordinance had a potential for serious accidents if not properly enforced. The ordinance identified busy streets that alternative vehicles would be required to traverse, Eyer said, putting them in their most vulnerable position with no side-impact protection, airbags, or door. “Simple physics, recipe of disaster should collisions occur, brought into being opportunity for injury or death that did not exist before now,” he said. If the ordinance passed, “remember your ‘yes’ vote when you first hear of an accident involving a cart on the streets of Olney,” Eyer said. Lambird asked for a consensus on inspections. McLaughlin said he would feel better if the police department performed them because they knew what needed to be checked. It would be easier for a private business not to care as much, he said. “I don’t wanna say I don’t care either way, but I’m fine either way,” McLaughlin said. An audience member said his concern was streets in the Taylor and Doenges subdivisions would be blocked in by Hall and East streets. “We cannot get out.” McLaughlin said they could drive on Hall but had to turn at the next road. Lambird said the intent was for people to cross to the nearest road. Audience Member said all streets were terrible at certain times of day, but other times there was no traffic. Lambird said the greater danger was when traffic is light and someone drives 45 miles per hour. Henton suggested prohibiting use on Van and Hall during school-bus hours. Lambird said the simpler, the better. Fehrenbacher said if it got too complex, people wouldn’t know the rules. Lambird said they would use the page with the police inspections. “The fifth page five,” Fehrenbacher said. Lambird said they needed a page two as well. “I think there’s an extra page five in there that’s only distinguished by a spacing error,” Zuber said. “There should be only four page fives.” Lambird said they were adding “at nearest intersection. And which page five are we talking about? The third one?” “No, the fifth one,” Fehrenbacher said. “It is substantively the same as the second page five,” Zuber said. Lambird stated the wording for inspections as chief of police or designated representatives, and permit renewal by presenting previous inspection. Eyer said they were making decisions before it was passed. Lambird said all input was welcome, and asked if Eyer planned to vote yes for the ordinance. “No. I’ve made my peace,” Eyer said. An audience member said his moped could drive 20 on the highway with no sides. Eyer said that was more maneuverable. Audience member Morgan Henton said a cart was more visible. “Remember when you hear of an accident it all could have been avoided,” Eyer said. McLaughlin said anything the city could do, including building a playground, splash park or swimming pool, offered the potential for someone to get hurt. People who drive carts make a decision, he said. “They also have to take some responsibility for themselves to make that decision,” McLaughlin said, adding there is inherent risk in everything. Lambird said that a century ago, city officials were having the same discussion about allowing cars on the roads. Eyer said automobiles and trucks were made for roadways, and golf carts were made for golf courses. The average vehicle sold was a 4,200-pound F150, he said. Henton said she kept thinking of motorcycles. Eyer said those were more maneuverable. Henton asked if they could make Van and Hall similar to “when children are present” in school zones. “We can put anything in an ordinance that you want to put in,” Zuber said, but it would come down to the common sense and judgment of an officer at the scene at the time. Lambird asked what Henton wanted to vote on. “When children are present,” Henton said “That seems subjective,” Lambird said. “How’s that?” Henton asked. Lambird asked about children present in the middle of the summer. Paddock said it applied from 7:30 a.m.-4 p.m. on school days. When kids were in the building, the speed limit was 30, he said. Lambird said it applied when children were coming or reasonably believed to be leaving. Henton said she would like Van and Hall to be the same as a school zone. “I’m not in favor of that,” Fehrenbacher said. Lambird said they would vote on the changes, then vote on the whole. Yes votes for each page would approve only the change to the ordinance, not the ordinance itself, he said. Members unanimously approved having vehicles certified by the city, and proscribing use on several roads except to travel to the nearest intersection. Henton asked about making Van and Hall school zones. They were getting into something subjective that would cause issues for officers to figure out how to make it work, Lambird said. “I’m just telling you you’re making it a nightmare for us,” Paddock said. Zuber said they could make a timeframe exception to the prohibition. “Generally brevity is the best option,” Lambird said. The council approved keeping Hall and Van on the list of prohibited streets on a 3-2 vote. Lambird, Eyer, and Fehrenbacher voted yes; Henton and McLaughlin voted no. The council approved having inspections done by the chief of police or designated representatives on 4-1 vote with Fehrenbacher voting no. Lambird asked when the ordinance would take effect. Zuber said it would be effective when it passed, but no one could get a permit until decals and signage arrived. Paddock asked if the ordinance allowed only carts or UTVs. Lambird said it would allow both. Eyer asked if they determined how much signage was required. Henton said IDOT did not have specific rules, but stated signage was required. Lambird said they could post it at the town’s entrances. Paddock noted there were places in town that did not allow parking but lacked no-parking signs. The ordinance passed 3-2 with Lambird, Henton, and McLaughlin voting yes; Eyer and Fehrenbacher voted no. “Motion passes. And thank you for all of that,” Lambird said. (Votes unanimous unless indicated. Council members include Mayor Mark Lambird, Greg Eyer, Morgan Fehrenbacher, Belinda Henton, and John McLaughlin.)

            VFW presents initial plans for memorial, council members suggest alternate locations
            Mark Allen Editor Olney City Council heard a presentation on a proposed veterans memorial at its meeting Nov. 25 and attempted to steer proponents away from their preferred location. Veterans of Foreign Wars Commander Ryan Higginbotham presented a rough sketch of a Richland County Veterans Memorial, north of the drive at the west entrance to Olney City Park. The area would eliminate the “dilapidated fountain,” he said, and include branch flags, donor bricks, and a list of local soldiers who were killed in action. “This is actually for them more than anything,” Higginbotham said of KIAs. They discussed completing the memorial by Veterans Day 2020, but realized Memorial Day 2021 was more realistic. “We do not wanna rush this. We want it done right,” Higginbotham said. Councilman John McLaughlin asked if City Manager Allen Barker had any concerns. Barker said they had some preliminary discussions about prevailing wage. The area would be leased to the VFW until completed, then leased back to the city. Councilwoman Morgan Fehrenbacher said there were several large trees in that area of the park. Higginbotham said there were three big trees in the middle, one of them diseased, and another with a dead top. A few trees on the edges might stay, he said, adding that they would need the space and didn’t want the memorial covered in leaves. VFW Quartermaster Robert Ritter said they also didn’t want roots coming up through the memorial. It would include a new water feature, and branch service songs would play at the tops of hours, Higginbotham said. “We would like for people outside the community to come in and see this and see what we’ve done,” he said of the location right off Ill. 130. “Our goal is to represent every Richland County veteran at no cost to them,” Ritter said. Fehrenbacher asked if no decision could be made until they resolve the issue of prevailing wage. City Attorney Bart Zuber said that was correct. Councilwoman Belinda Henton said the city would be responsible for maintenance. Higginbotham said they planned to establish a foundation for repairs, “not just build it and say, ‘Here you go, Olney, it’s up to you.’” The memorial committee included five VFW members, Higginbotham said, and they planned to add four community members. Ritter said there were people willing to donate labor, which could not be donated to the city. Henton said the proposed area included five mature oaks. “Do you have any idea how long it takes an oak tree to bear acorns?” she asked. Higginbotham asked how long. Henton said 20-30 years. Higginbotham said they would be more than happy to replace trees. “How big? Twenty years old?” Henton asked. Higginbotham said no, but they would be willing to do something for Arbor Day. Henton said the value of those trees couldn’t be replaced. “The value of our veterans is higher than the value of a tree,” McLaughlin said. Ritter said the area was not struggling for trees or food for wildlife. Henton said there were already several memorials, including big ones at the courthouse. Higginbotham said that if you blinked, you wouldn’t know the courthouse memorials were there. Henton said the proposed memorial was at ground level. Higginbotham said it included a 12-foot memorial. Henton asked if they considered someplace in Musgrove Park. They said that park was saved for sports. “In my view, that’s for the children,” Ritter said, adding that not many people drive by Musgrove. Henton asked if they had seen the park during baseball tournaments, adding that Mack Avenue and East Street were major thoroughfares. “And there’s no trees. That’s my point.” Fehrenbacher said prevailing wage remained an issue. Ritter said the preferred location was about a half acre. Henton asked if the white-squirrel memorial would be removed. Higginbotham said it would be relocated, and Ritter said the front of the Fair Board building would be a good location. Councilman Greg Eyer said he absolutely supported and applauded the memorial, adding that he was a veteran. “Site selection I do question also,” he said. Trees could be replaced, but his grandchildren wouldn’t be able to enjoy trees that big. They might have some issues but seemed to be in fair condition, Eyer said. That area of the park was used a lot by people stopping to eat lunch, he said, enjoying the shade and easy access. The fountain was dilapidated but had been operating recently. “For children of Olney, we’ve grown up with that,” Eyer said. “We’d make our parents go out of the way to see that fountain.” Olney City Park and Musgrove Park each had 50 acres, he said, and there might be other sites that would not require destroying a developed, landscaped, and utilized area. Eyer asked if they would consider any other areas. “We would have to discuss that,” Higginbotham said. McLaughlin said he had no problem with the proposed area and hardly even saw anyone there. Eyer asked if the city had plans for the location of the Musgrove house. Mayor Mark Lambird said a picnic area was the idea. “That’d be pretty hidden from any roadways,” Ritter said. Lambird said that area had several trees, but the maples were prone to blowing over, and most oaks would probably die within 10 years due to disease. Ritter said the fountain needed to be dug up and repaired because the base was leaking. Barker said he had not heard that but confirmed its condition. “It is in pretty bad lookin’ shape right now.” Lambird said they needed to research how they could proceed regardless of location. “So I reckon we’ll see you folks again at the December-January meeting.” Fehrenbacher suggested looking at other options. Higginbotham said they could and would. Ritter said other places they considered were privately owned, which would cause problems. The college’s land adjacent to the highway would be ideal, he said, but he didn’t know how hard it would be to get them to allow it. “They have an oil derrick,” Henton said. She said northern Musgrove Park had the skateboard park and children’s memorial, plus a parking lot and playground, which would attract visitors. Eyer said the southwest corner was leveled for a field that never materialized. “It’s been used as a soccer field,” McLaughlin said. Ritter said they wanted a location that already had utilities.

            Council taps Plan Commission to consider cannabis zoning
            Mark Allen Editor The city’s Plan Commission will consider zoning rules pertaining to adult-use cannabis facilities, Olney City Council decided at its meeting Nov. 12. City Manager Allen Barker said zoning regulations did not include cannabis, and if it were to be sold, the city needed proper zoning. The Plan Commission would hold a public hearing, he said. City Attorney Bart Zuber said the council previously decided that if sales were allowed, the city would apply the maximum tax rate allowed by the state. He said the council now had to decide whether to allow sales. The state did not allow prohibiting sale of medical cannabis, but the city could prohibit recreational sales or regulate the time, place of manner of sales, including zoning districts, Zuber said. He said probable zones for cannabis sales included C-1 Neighborhood Shopping District, C-2 Commercial District, C-3 Central Business District, C-4 Planned Commercial District, M-1 Light Industrial District, and M-2 Heavy Industrial District. The state designated six types of cannabis businesses, Zuber said, including dispensaries, cultivation centers, and transporters. A transporter might located in M-1, and a dispensary in C-1 or C-3, he said, which the commission would address with a public hearing. Zuber tweaked the model ordinance to be compatible with city code. City Clerk Kelsie Sterchi said the hearing needed to be set by noon the next day, and all the council needed that night was a resolution to call a public hearing. The commission could make a recommendation on prohibition or where to allow sales, she said. Councilman Greg Eyer asked if it were a matter of decorum for the Plan Commission to hold a hearing. “My thought, I am not for this, not for sale of recreational cannabis use in Olney.” If other members were also in opposition, there was no reason to forward the issue to the commission, he said. Mayor Mark Lambird noted the council just finished a lengthy discussion of the need for additional tax revenue to improve roads. “I think we would be remiss to think we can stand here and hold back the ocean. It’s coming. People are going to be buying marijuana in Olney, and they’re going to smoke it,” he said. “I would prefer they buy it here, and I would prefer every community in the area banned it,” Lambird said. Eyer questioned whether legal sales would be cheaper than what people get from dealers. “I don’t think anyone knows,” said Police Chief Kevin Paddock. Lambird said he talked with a Colorado town, approximately Olney’s size, near the New Mexico border. “The amount of tax revenue they’re were generated was gigantic,” he said, about $3 million a year. While Olney’s experience might differ, Lambird said, “The morality of it is, if you don’t like it, don’t do it. We can’t stop them. We need jobs, we need revenue.” Councilwoman Morgan Fehrenbacher said nothing would stop such businesses from locating inside the county, so the city could miss the tax dollars and still have nearby cannabis sales. Lambird said communities that opt out would be allowing it in five to 10 years. “That’s just the way it’s going to work. It’s coming.” Only two dispensaries would be allowed in the region in the first round, Lambird said, so while Olney’s chances were not great, they were improving daily when other communities opted out. Councilman John McLaughlin said he didn’t mind the commission holding a hearing and preparing recommendations. “I’m more leaning on the ‘no’ side like Greg. We’ll see.”

            $2 million spreadsheet gaffe boosts RCHS overrun to $6 million
            Mark Allen Editor The discovery of a $2 million clerical error pushed bids for renovation of Richland County High School to $6 million above the original estimate of $25.5 million, the board of education was told at a special meeting Nov. 5. Jon Carroll, of Poettker Construction, said previous spreadsheets failed to include nearly $2 million in fees for construction-management services, and the error was caught Nov. 4. As a result, the total was $31.5 million instead of $29.5 million. “That’s very disappointing,” said board member Steve Marrs. “It’s extremely disappointing,” Carroll said, adding that board members were “not as disappointed as I am.” A “simple formula error” resulted from transferring the wrong number from the budget to the spreadsheet, Carroll said. They had been focused on money-saving revisions, he said, which was why the mistake hadn’t been found. “Not one of our prouder moments, Steve,” Carroll said. “So now we can’t afford this,” Marrs said. “That’s where we’re at right now,” Carroll said, but they had to work to get the cost down. He mentioned discussing with RCHS Principal Chad LeCrone and Superintendent Chris Simpson about what additional funding the board might be comfortable providing. About $1 million had been trimmed by tweaking the renovation of the T&I wing, Carroll said, and an additional $250,000 might be saved from changes in bids already received. Cuts or changes to materials and finishes would be considered. “That’s a lot of changes for 5 million dollars,” said board member Dennis Anderson. President Jeff Wilson asked what caused the large differences in estimates and bids for mechanical and electrical. Carroll said he didn’t think those budgets were right to begin with. “Why?” Wilson asked. Carroll said they did $4 million in initial value engineering, but did not realize that much savings when bids were received. The estimates were supposed to be conservative, he said, but “the market kind of said otherwise.” Some bids were higher than anticipated, Carroll said, but bad numbers were included from how the scope evolved through design such as not being able to reuse ducts. “Some of that scope crept up.” Wilson asked if BLDD or Poettker did that. Carroll said they worked with BLDD on technical systems, and the architects gave Poettker the original scope and a budget prepared before Poettker was involved. As the project went through design-development, they tried to get feedback from each designer on changes to the scope, Carroll said, but didn’t get good information or ask the right questions to track changes. Once drawings were distributed, they realized the project had a larger scope. Bidders said a lot more work was involved than anticipated, he said. “There was some miscommunication between us and the design team before the bids went out,” Carroll said. The market for construction work was also higher, and the project received less interest than expected, he said. “The big question now is how do we save 6 million,” said board member Leon Redman, adding it couldn’t be done by tweaking the design or floor coverings. “It has to be something that’s a fifth of the project,” he said. Carroll said Poettker had not been tasked with saving $6 million at that point, but to get the scope the district wanted to achieve, that’s what the market was stating it would cost. Anderson said changes would have to be big to get the cost down that much, and tweaking the scope or schedule would not cut it. Redman said the whole project needed to be reshaped in some aspects, and the district was committed to part of it already. He asked what the options were. Carroll said it depended on how many dollars needed to be saved. Anderson asked how much the new theater cost on its own. Carroll said he did not remember a total price for the addition. Wilson noted the addition included the commons area. Carroll said the auxiliary gym would be the other new work. Anderson asked how much the gym added. Carroll said he didn’t know. “So you don’t have information for us to make a decision tonight,” Marrs said. Carroll said the question was how much more the district was willing to add to the project, and cutting a million was a lot different from $3 million. Redman said the board’s previously approved $27.276 million was a good place to start; they had already increased the project by $1.7 million. Somehow, $4.3 million needed to be cut, he said. Wilson said the $6 million amount over budget included $1 million in contingencies. Redman said they would need that and possibly more. “Jon, I’m sorry but I gotta say this, the confidence is gone. It’s gone,” Wilson said. “We as a board have to come to consensus how much dollars we’re comfortable putting to the project from our own dollars,” Wilson said. Board member Cindy Lockley said they discussed using money from Buildings and Grounds. Wilson said the district had more sales-tax money than it needed to immediately service bonds, a strong Working Cash fund of $2.8 million that had not been touched for 13-15 years, and about $4 million in Buildings and Grounds. That last fund was built up knowing the high school needed work, he said. Simpson said $600,000 of the $1.3 million generated by sales tax was obligated for bond payments, leaving $700,000 that could be applied to the $6 million; the board previously approved an extra $1.7 million, dropping the amount to $4.3 million, and the sales tax money got that to $3.6 million. If they tasked Poettker with finding another million, the total would be $2.6 million with a projected $4.1 million in FY21 in Buildings and Grounds and $2.7 million in Working Cash that could be applied to preserve the project’s overall scope. The district had a window of opportunity to plan a building that would be utilized for 50-70 years, Simpson said. They needed to decide what additional amount they were comfortable contributing. The district’s goal and commitment was to deliver the renovation without impacting property-tax rates, Simpson said. “And none of what I just described” impacts tax rates, he said. The district’s fund balances were accrued to spend money on the high school, he said, adding that using them would not impact the Education Fund. “If you’re looking for a recommendation from me, we need to add additional money to the project to get the scope of project we want and accomplish goals for facilities for the next three generations of students,” Simpson said. The question of how much was probably $2.5-$3 million, he said. Anderson noted the money was saved for fixing part of the building that was torn down. Wilson said the gym roof would probably have been the bigger expense, and that was now part of the renovation project. Simpson said Sliva Auditorium had probably needed $1.7 million of work. “I dunno, it’s starting to look cheaper than new now,” Anderson said. Board member Kelsie Barnes asked Carroll if another $1 million could realistically be cut from the project. Carroll said he thought they could get it from $6 million over budget to $5 million with minimal scope changes. Changing flooring and ceilings outside T&I had not yet been considered, he said. Lockley said it was fine to go cheaper on ceilings and floors because they could be updated in the future, but she didn’t want to cut items in classrooms that teachers needed. Carroll said changes in finishes would not change functionality, and some items might be more durable and less expensive. They had been focused on mechanical systems but didn’t realize there was such a large gap to bridge, he said. LeCrone said they had just received the numbers that day, and needed time to study projections. Wilson said he hated to lose the safety net the district’s fund balances had built up, but the money came from taxpayers. “If we’re not putting it into our students, then we need to change our tax levy,” he said. “I think we’re gonna get to. I don’t think there’s an option,” Anderson said, adding that they had not started building the main part and had problems with extras. Lockley said that if all the money were spent on the renovation, the district wouldn’t have it for work on RCES. Simpson said he talked with State Rep. Darren Bailey recently about the future of education funding, but couldn’t get State Sen. Dale Righter to return his calls, probably because Righter was not running for re-election. Wilson asked how much the state owed the district during each fiscal year. Simpson said state payments for the Education Fund were kept mostly up to date, but categorical payments lagged for transportation and special education. Simpson said Working Cash levied about $140,000 each year, and was up to $2.609 million after not being touched since at least 2002. Operating funds increased from $5.3 million in FY15 to $14.5 million in FY19, he said. Wilson said the board needed to know what the district’s financial rating would be if fund balances were used. The district had $700,000 of sales-tax revenue more than required to service debt, he said, and could have another $600,000 before the project was completed. In addition to using money from Working Cash, and Buildings and Grounds, the Education Fund could be tapped for items such as furnishings, Wilson said. “It’s a healthy fund balance now. I don’t like the thought of invading it,” he said, but they needed to consider all angles to determine how much each fund could be drawn down, and what that would do to the district’s financial profile. Simpson said numbers presented at the meeting were rough figures prepared that afternoon, and better totals would be compiled for a variety of scenarios. Furnishings would be an allowable and appropriate use of the Education Fund, he said, because they directly impact classroom structure, similar to buying materials, supplies, and equipment. Wilson said Education money could buy welders for the ag shop. “Things like that fit. And those are fairly significant items.” He added that he hoped the Education Association would see those dollars as being used to improve the teaching environment. “We’ll communicate with the association along the way,” Simpson said. Redman asked Carroll how much could be trimmed from the bids without making major changes. Carroll said cutting more than $1 million would require significant changes. “Beyond a million, there’s just not enough square-foot dollars out there.” After reviewing all changes made during value engineering, the board: Awarded a $316,000 contract for Masonry to Wagner Masonry; Awarded a $660,515 contract for Roofing and Sheet Metal to Geissler Roofing; Awarded a $996,240 contract for Aluminum Storefront and Curtain Wall Systems to Illini Glass Solutions, LLC dba Arrow Glass & Mirror.

            City approves just-in-case cannabis tax
            Mark Allen Editor Olney City Council approved a 3-percent tax on recreational cannabis at its meeting Sept. 23 after confirming the move was not tantamount to approving a dispensary to locate in the city. City Attorney Bart Zuber presented a model ordinance suggested by Illinois Municipal League. Recreational cannabis will be legal Jan. 1; as the law was written, cities could not apply a tax until Dec. 1, he said, but IML believed that would change. The League advised approving a tax now to get the ordinance certified by the Department of Revenue, said City Treasurer Jane Guinn. If a tax were not approved by Oct. 1, Zuber said, it could not be applied until July 1 or Sept. 1, 2020. A dispensary would likely not propose a location for several months, he said. The council could choose not to approve a recreational dispensary, but the state did not allow cities to reject medical dispensaries if the state approved them. “Those are two very distinct things,” Zuber said of medical versus recreational. The city could approve fairly strict zoning rules, he said, and recounted what he recently learned in Chicago. Patrons enter through a bulletproof breezeway and present identification, then enter a waiting area, Zuber said. All areas were under constant surveillance, and the state police have live access to all surveillance feeds. After traversing a vestibule, customers another waiting area, with the retail space in still another room, he said. The number of people was limited, and no product was ever allowed on the retail floor, although customers could peruse packaging. Sales representatives enter a secure area akin to a bank vault, Zuber said, then proceed back through many locked areas. “It is really, really strict.” Adult-recreational venues were one of the law’s categories, he said. Others include craft growers, infusers, transporters, and cultivation centers. The city could host a company involved solely in transport, Zuber said. “It’s an industry that even the experts are saying we’ll take it one step at a time,” he said. “The consensus is Illinois has the most regulated yet counterintuitively most efficient system in the nation,” Zuber said. Councilman John McLaughlin asked for clarification that approving the tax would not mean approving dispensaries. Zuber said the ordinance meant that if the council approved a dispensary, and one located here – which he said was a “bigger if” – the city would receive up to 3 percent of gross receipts. McLaughlin asked if the 3 percent would be earmarked by the state for some purpose. Guinn said it would not, but there were other taxes added for training and other reasons. Zuber said the state would be collecting that money anyway. “There’s gonna be a lotta tax on all these products.” Audience member Bruno Biava asked what was the highest tax the city could apply. Told 3 percent, he said, “Heavens to Murgatroyd. That’s incredible.” In other business: • The council declined to amend an ordinance to allow parking on the south side of Elm Street between Kitchell and Camp avenues. City Manager Allen Barker said the request was received “more or less for old-folk parking for businesses in that area.” Police Chief Kevin Paddock said eliminating parking on the north side might be necessary to create more space if parking were allowed on the south side. Mayor Mark Lambird said the request was to have parking on the south rather than the north. McLaughlin said navigating the road would be tough with parking on north and south. Lambird said they wanted to eliminate parking on the north side of the road. “That’s not what’s presented here,” McLaughlin said. Lambird said he was stating exactly what the business requested. McLaughlin asked what the difference was between parking on the north or south sides. Lambird said the north side was divided by entrances, and parking was probably not allowed on the south side originally due to Central School. Barker said the south side was currently marked for no parking. McLaughlin said the rest of the street from Camp to Ill. 130 would still be no parking on the south side, and questioned allowing it for one block. “How frequently would that be relevant?” Eyer asked of the restaurant/club parking lot being full. Lambird said he didn’t know but sees cars parked on the north side. Fehrenbacher said the change would not allow many additional parking spots. Lambird said someone measured it. McLaughlin said it would probably add two spots because there were only two drives on the north side. Henton said people can park on the west side of Kitchell near the school. “It was just a request they made as things go along,” Lambird said. McLaughlin said it didn’t make a lot of sense to “chop up the road like that.” Fehrenbacher said she didn’t think the change would allow many more parking spaces. • Barker said Eyer requested information on adding shading to “Olney” when the Industrial Park water tower was repainted. City Engineer Mike Bridges got a quote of $7,000 per side, Barker said. “You still want it?” Fehrenbacher asked. “No, thank you. We’re good,” Eyer said. • The council approved selling or recycling 47 bicycles held by Olney Police Department. “Where did we come across 47 bicycles?” McLaughlin asked. Paddock said the department accumulated 50 to 100 a year. Barker said bicycles are just found laying around. McLaughlin asked how that was advertised to someone who had a bike stolen. “If you call the police department, we can tell you,” Paddock said. Barker added that if someone filed an incident report, the department could contact them. Paddock said they used to repair bicycles and give them to children, but the bikes were frequently ridden off and dumped somewhere, and the Street Department would pick them up again.(Votes unanimous unless indicated. Council members include Mayor Mark Lambird, Greg Eyer, Morgan Fehrenbacher, Belinda Henton, and John McLaughlin.)

            Fred Kuenstler, 83
            Fred Kuenstler, 83, of Olney, died Thursday, October 10, 2019, at Lavender Ridge in Olney. Mass of Christian Burial was held Oct. 15 at te St. Joseph Catholic Church in Olney. Burial was in St. Joseph Catholic Cemetery. Fred was born October 23, 1935, in Richland County, the son of Henry and Georgiana (Rudolphi) Kuenstler. Fred married Shirley (Shryock) on December 28, 1961, at St. Joseph Catholic Church in Olney. She survives. Fred was president of the board of directors of Prairie Farms for more than 20 years, and he owned and operated Domino K Dairy. He was a Parish Council Trustee, and he was on the St. Joseph School Board. Fred was a Fourth Degree member of Knights of Columbus. He was also part of the Clay City Class of 1952, and a member of St. Joseph Catholic Church. Fred’s first priority was always his wife. Beyond her, his life revolved around his faith, his farm, and his family. Fred is survived by his wife of 58 years, Shirley Kuenstler, of Olney; son, John Kuenstler and wife Jill, of Los Angeles; daughter, Kerry Kuenstler, of Arlington, Texas; grandchildren, Daisy and Luke Kuenstler, of Los Angeles, and Justin and Jacob Constantin, of Arlington, Texas; sister, Karen (Tom) Zuber, of Claremont; brothers, Jim (Peg) Kuenstler, Richard (Jeanette) Kuenstler, Dan (MaryAnn) Kuenstler, and Bill (Rita) Kuenstler; numerous nieces and nephews; and beloved brother-in-law, Don Dallmier. He was preceded in death by his parents; infant son, Frederick Kuenstler; and sisters, Ruth Bene and husband John, LouAnn Dallmier, June McGeehon and husband Bill, and Norma Eugenia.

            Daniel F. Gaddy, 75
            Daniel F. Gaddy, 75 Daniel F. Gaddy, 75, of Olney, died Monday, October 7, 2019, at Helia Healthcare in Olney. Cremation rites were accorded. No formal services were planned. Daniel was born November 10, 1943, in Olney, the son of Homer and Marie (Cochran) Gaddy. He married Camille Knackmuhs on August 25, 1968, in Olney. She survives. He worked as a factory worker for several years. He served in the United States Air Force, and attended Free Methodist Church in Olney. Daniel enjoyed listening to music and taking walks, and was in a bowling league in Olney. He will be sadly missed by his family, friends, and everyone who knew him. Daniel is survived by his wife, Camille Gaddy, of Olney; three sons, Daniel Thomas Gaddy, of Olney, Homer Roy Gaddy, (Pat) of Olney, Ronald Dale Gaddy, of Olney; brother, Tom Gaddy, of Olney; and sister, Helen Fisk, of Olney. He was preceded in death by his parents, Homer and Marie Gaddy, and brother, Dale Gaddy.
